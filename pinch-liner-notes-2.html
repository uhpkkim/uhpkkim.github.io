"Hello intrepid listener! Thank you for listening to PINCH, the first compilation release by Tin Mitt Records. If you don’t already know, every single track on PINCH is ~exactly~ one minute long (1:00). This was the only constraint/prompt given to participants (along with the title) and everyone responded to it in vastly different ways. I could not possibly be more grateful to all of the artists who submitted work to this album! Of course it wouldn’t exist without everyone’s tracks, but it wouldn’t be so incredible without everyone’s tracks being really really really cool. I highly recommend that you listen to the full album in its entirety without skipping tracks because these artists made brand new original tracks specifically for this compilation. Take breaks if you need to. Heck, download it and put it on shuffle or make up your own track order if you want!

This album took about a year from the time of conception to release, a lot of that time was due to me getting COVID for the first time literally on the day of the submission deadline, which took me a few months to recover from.

Most of the artists on this album are from occupied Piscataway land known colonially as Baltimore MD, but there’s a good handful of scattered folks from all around the globe. Not all of the artists on this album are queer but damn sure as heck most of them are, and yknow what? like half of them are even trans.

Anyway. Thank you. Please enjoy this weird album. We all worked hard on it."
- PK

THE BENEFITS OF
A 1:00 SONG LIFESTYLE

"I didn't come up with the idea of an album of exclusively one-minute-long songs. PINCH was directly inspired by both The Commercial Album (by The Residents, 1980) and Whack World (by Tierra Whack, 2018), both of which are entirely made up of minute-long songs. It wasn't until part-way through production of PINCH that Tildas (album contributor) introduced me to Morgan Fisher's Miniatures compilations (1980, 2000).

I first adapted this time limit idea into a Frogsmog solo album of 100-second songs. Through that, I found that having an arbitrary short time limitation allowed me to really focus in sharply on a single idea. There's no room to aimlessly wander off, and no room to slowly establish things. The essence of the song gets compressed and becomes highly potent. Anything added to the song becomes just as important as every other part. No part really fades into the background. Everything is highly present. It's a very satisfying way to approach songwriting and I wanted to share this joy with others by forcing them to adhere to a strict time limit.

The benefits a one (1) minute-long song compilation are clear. New less experienced artists are given an easy goal to work towards. More experienced artists can make something without worrying about larger albums and overarching projects. Everyone on this album is on the same level playing field. Everyone gets the exact same time in the spotlight. A 1:00 song is sturdy. A 1:00 stands strong on its own, but even stronger amongst its kin.

If I was more poetic I might say that the reason I chose the theme and time limit was to commentate / reflect on any number of big important aspects about the world we currently live in (urgency, the way time is used to oppress, infinite growth and excess consumption, planned obsolescence, endless short-form social media feeds, the ticking clock of global disasters, mortality, etc etc) but really I just thought the time limit would be funny. However, some artists did use the theme to reflect on these larger topics and so many more. And WOW I thank them for it. They gave my silly little gimmick a depth I would have never imagined on my own. I think in the end, this album is so many things. Please attribute any interpretation to this album that you feel is fitting."
-PK

Deep wizard theory analysis for the true freaks:
In lieu of lyrics, and in the interest of transparency in an artform/culture that seems to love being cryptic and secretive (much like alchemists), here is an overview of the structure of this track. It's cool if you don't understand what's going on here - it's some advanced deep nerd garbage for turbo nerds. I hope it can at least inspire someone to learn more about music theory concepts. Feel free to hit me up if you wanna talk more about this nonsense! I promise I'm not intimidating! I also like writing simple pop music I swear!

Meter/time signature: The intro is in 4/4, then breaks down to a 6/4 groove, followed by a back-and-forth call and response between the established 6/4 rhythm (guitar+banjo lines) and the established 4/4 rhythm (horn lines). After the organ transition, it all melds together into a stable 6/4 (where the horns and strings join together). At least, that's how I was thinking of it, you could probably interpret the meter a few different ways.

Key/scale: The beginning is just in Ab dorian (the second mode of Gb major). But in the second half, the song goes through a cycle of chords. Each time there's a new chord, it acts as the tonal center for a new mode/scale that the melody and bass follow. This is directly influenced by jazz chord-scale theory techniques (but used compositionally instead of improvisationally). These mode changes happen fluidly - often before the chord they're connected to shows up. It was all written linearly - building the chord progression was informed by the modes being used, and visa versa. There were several layers of internal logic going on that I'm not going to go into right now. If you want to follow the chord changes, listen for Greg's autoharp.

These chords / modes are:
Abm (dorian) B (lydian) Gb (lydian) Ab (mixolydian)
Ebm7 (dorian) Bbm (phrygian b4) Bm (harmonic major) Db (mixolydian).

At least, that’s how I was thinking about it when I wrote it. Since many of the mode changes are in the same relative key, you could also interpret it as simply changing key 3 times: Ab dorian, Ab mixolydian, Bb phrygian b4. Or something like that. Listen. After a certain point I just let the song go where it wanted to go. Melodically, nothing -really- repeats during the second half, but all of the horn lines are transformations of the original horn melody motif from the intro. Golly, okay, thanks for reading, I hope someone found this interesting??"
